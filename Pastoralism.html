Pastoralism and Archaeology:

(The following is an excerpt from a manuscript I am currently writing)

The archaeology of mobile pastoralists has been in large part confounded by researchers’ misconceptions about the nature of mobile pastoralism as a subsistence system. Researchers often misconstrue what it means to be a mobile pastoralist, and often do not closely examine their assumptions about what kinds of things go along with being a mobile pastoralist. The issue is that mobile pastoralists are not just mobile, they are also pastoralists (Cribb 1991), and this by necessity means they are doing different things than people who are mobile but not pastoralists, or who raise animals but are not mobile. Accepting this as the most basic dimension of mobile pastoralism will allow us to examine the interaction of various aspects of mobility and pastoralism across case studies to gain a greater understanding of exactly how mobile pastoralists interact with their environment.

In order for mobile pastoral groups to make pastoral production viable, they must be flexible. Because the various dimensions of pastoral landuse are not necessarily formulaically tied to one another, they cannot be separated into a series of monolithic types. This idea has not yet been fully integrated into the literature on pastoralists, and many modern studies still try to classify pastoral peoples into types such “nomadic”, “semi-nomadic”, or “transhumant” (eg. Barfield 1993; Bartosiewicz and Greenfield 1999; de Villiers and Hirtle 2002; FAO 2001; Khazanov 1994, 2001; Khazanov, et al. 2001; Levy 1992). Although there is some sense in the literature that these types are fairly fluid, only Cribb (1991) explicitly states that there exists a continuum between these types.

Cribb discusses the variable ways that these terms have been employed in great detail (1991, pp. 18-21) and I will not repeat them here. I wish to reiterate, however, that the greatest problem with the typological approach is that the act of typifying necessarily removes the actions being typified from the context in which they operate whilst simultaneously simplifying their complex natures to make them fit the type. This severely limits the scales at which these behaviors can be studied and is the root of many of the misconceptions about mobile pastoralism. The issue is that each of the different parts of a pastoral landuse strategy can be localized or dispersed, intensified or moderate, seasonal or year-round, and can be integrated at the household, local group, tribe, or region (Cribb 1991). The variables dynamically relate to each other as well as the environment, technology, social structure, and the political context surrounding the group, and all of these things can vary over time and space.

One can think of pastoralism as single subsistence strategy that encompasses a very wide range of variation. Much like the well known forager–collector continuum (Binford 1980) and more complex models of hunter-gatherer mobility (Kelly 1992; Whallon 2006), we can begin to deconstruct the idea of pastoral subsistence into a series of dimensions. Cribb (1991) has so far been the only researcher to attempt this. He created a nomadic–sedentary continuum for pastoralists which he compared in a bi-axial plot to another continuum of the degree of reliance on pastoral production (Cribb 1991, Fig. 2.1). Although he did not specifically dimensionalize other aspects of pastoral subsistence in this same graphical manner, he touches on many important aspects that can also be plotted along similar continua.

The most durable physical evidence for highly mobile pastoralists in West Asia and Africa are circles of stones that once anchored brush corrals in conjunction with other stone outlined features that are the remains of temporary dwellings (Banning 1993; Betts 1982, 1992; Chang and Koster 1986; Cribb 1991; Eddy, et al. 1999; Kinahan 1991; Sadr 1991; Saidel 2001; Simms 1988; Smith 1983; Andrew B Smith 1992a; Smith 2005; Zarins 1992). The pattern of settlements across the landscape has been used to make arguments about the types of pastoral activities that were occurring in the area (Betts 1982, 1988, 1992). The soil inside the circles has been found to have a unique chemical signature, especially greatly elevated phosphate and nitrogen levels, due to the large amounts of dung and urine that gets concentrated in them (Winnett 1957). For the same reasons, there are also high amounts of pollen and phytoliths in these soils (Zohar 1992). Beyond these circular stone enclosures, one may also find evidence in rock art and graffito left by ancient nomads, as well as cemeteries and shrines in very dispersed locations (Köhler-Rollefson 1989a, b).

Cribb (1991) describes the site of Tepe Tula’i which he argues is a mobile pastoralist campsite dating to 6,200 BC. He makes this determination based on the layout and character of the architecture, which conforms to his model of mobile pastoral dwellings, as well as faunal evidence. Smith (2005) and Sadr (1997) examine the frequency of pottery types in southern Africa to differentiate the campsites of ancient mobile pastoralists from hunter-gatherers. They were then able to discus the chronology and nature of early pastoralism in the region. Banning use ethnoarchaeological models to identify the remains of ancient mobile pastoralists in northern Jordan(Banning 1993, 1995). He was able to identify stone platforms in the upper layers of the site of Tabaqat al-Buma that may relate to storing milk products. While these examples show that the archaeology of mobile pastoralists can be analyzed archaeologically, it is still in general very difficult to build a picture of how intensely a given area was utilized by highly mobile pastoralists at a given time in the past from conventional archaeological arguments alone.

On the other end of the mobility continuum, it is easier to find evidence for intensity of pastoralism at more sedentary sites in the Near East. This has mainly been done through the analysis of faunal assemblages from major sites. The faunal record from ‘Ayn Ghazal has been especially amenable to this type of analysis. We can see trends in herd size, herd composition, and changes in the ratio between wild and domestic animal remains that indicate that people at ‘Ayn Ghazal and other early Neolithic sites were intensifying their reliance on domestic flocks and pastoral production through time (Rollefson 1997; Rollefson and Köhler-Rollefson 1989; Wasse 2002; Wiedemann, et al. 1999). At ‘Ayn Ghazal this pastoral intensification combined with other types land-use intensification and the localized environmental degradation that resulted may have been the reason why the site was basically abandoned at the end of the Neolithic period (Behnke 1980; Lancaster and Lancaster 1990).

We can do more with the archaeological evidence we have for ancient mobile pastoralists than is allowed under current frameworks of pastoral archaeology. The types of data that we already collect can all be used to approximate the location of ancient mobile pastoral groups on the different plots used in this paper. Herd compositions can be assessed from faunal analysis, the amount of agricultural foods consumed can be assessed by paleobotanical analysis, mobility patterns can be assessed through architectural analysis and settlement patterns, and exchange of material cultural items can be assessed by regional typological studies. Using the dimensional framework presented in this paper allows us to make hypotheses about ancient pastoral landuse that have explicit test implications. Because we know how differences in pastoral mobility and production affect modern landscapes, we can use the heuristic devices presented in figures 1 thru 7 to compare ancient mobile pastoralists with our ethnographic and historic cases to draw conclusions about how different ancient pastoralists were impacting the landscape.

In general, the issue of mobile pastoral landuse is complex. There have been numerous attempts to understand pastoralism in economic, social, political, and environmental contexts. Most of these attempts suffer from a lack of organization of the concepts with which they try to build their cases. The misuse and abuse of jargony terms with regard to mobility and production are the main culprits here, and this paper has attempted to address the misuse of these concepts. Thinking of these phenomena in terms of dimensions of variability aids our ability to understand how and why different pastoral groups practice pastoralism differently.

When these frameworks are applied to ethnographic and historic case studies, some general patterns become apparent. First, groups who live in more marginal environments are more residentially mobile, move further in their seasonal rounds, are more apt towards nomadic movement of these seasonal rounds through time, are more focused on pastoral production, and specialize in desert adapted pastoral species, but are more reliant on groups outside the marginal zone for both material cultural items and subsistence. More sedentary groups are found in better environments, and are more prone to agriculture as a significant portion of their productive strategy, and are less apt to be nomadic over time. They are also more apt to be self sufficient in terms of food and material culture. Groups who are more logistically mobile tend to focus on animals that are not as hardy, but which are easier to manage. They are not quite as nomadic as the more residentially mobile pastoral groups, but never-the-less experience some degree of nomadic drift. They often practice agriculture as part of their productive strategy, but still rely to some degree on exchange to round out their diet and fill their material cultural needs

These frameworks can also help us assess the potential impacts of different pastoral landuse strategies on specific environments. One would be quite correct to state that pastoral land-use can have serious harmful impacts on the landscape, but this is not always the case. Some very sensitive marginal environments have supported sustained pastoral exploitation for very long periods of time. Poor policy and management decisions emplaced on pastoral peoples by modern states is acting to upset many of the traditional systems of pastoral land-tenure and careful territorial buffering that have been used for centuries as ways of reducing the impacts of intensive pastoralism in these areas. It is therefore more important than ever to find evidence of ancient pastoralists and the effects of different types of pastoral land-use on ancient landscapes. This evidence can help corroborate the kinds of things we are learning from modern traditional pastoralists, and help us learn to mitigate the kinds of degradation we are noticing in marginal rangelands today.
